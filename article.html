Essay · Physics & Philosophy

Richard Feynman 12 min read Physics

Nature does not owe us comprehension. The laws of physics emerged from a universe that preceded human consciousness by thirteen billion years, and they were not designed with our intuition in mind. The electron does not care whether we find its behaviour strange. The photon does not wait for us to agree on what it is. And yet, despite this, we have learned to predict, to measure, and to describe — even when we cannot truly understand.

Our brains evolved on the savannah, tracking objects at low speeds, judging distances in three dimensions, anticipating trajectories under constant gravity. We are superbly equipped for the world of medium-sized objects moving at modest velocities. But nature, it turns out, does not confine itself to that narrow regime.

At the scale of atoms, the familiar categories dissolve. A particle is not here or there — it is described by a probability amplitude that simultaneously occupies every permitted path. The moment we measure it, the wave function collapses. The moment we stop looking, it spreads out again. No metaphor from everyday life cleanly captures this. Not waves on water. Not billiard balls. Not fog.

"If you think you understand quantum mechanics, you don't understand quantum mechanics."

This is not a failure of physics. It is a confrontation with the limits of language — a language built for a world that quantum theory now tells us is only approximate.

Consider the double-slit experiment. A beam of electrons is fired at a barrier with two narrow openings. On the screen behind, an interference pattern accumulates — the signature of waves. But electrons are particles. How does a single particle pass through both slits?

The answer quantum mechanics offers is unsatisfying in the most profound way: the electron has no definite position until it is detected. It travels as a probability cloud, interferes with itself, and only upon measurement is forced to commit to a single location. Close one slit — even after the electron has already passed — and the interference vanishes.

The universe, it seems, maintains multiple possibilities in superposition and resolves them only at the last possible moment. Whether this represents genuine physical indeterminacy or simply our ignorance of deeper variables remains one of the deepest open questions in science.

Special relativity is no less strange. The speed of light is constant for all observers — not just approximately, but exactly. A photon emitted by a source moving toward you at half the speed of light still arrives at c, not 1.5c. Velocity does not add the way a moving train and a thrown ball add.

The consequence is that time itself becomes flexible. A clock aboard a fast-moving spacecraft ticks more slowly than an identical clock at rest. Two events that are simultaneous in one reference frame are not simultaneous in another. Space and time are not separate stages on which physics plays out — they are a single fabric, warped by mass and energy, and the geometry of that fabric is gravity.

"Space tells matter how to move. Matter tells space how to curve."
· · ·

The observable universe — the sphere of space from which light has had time to reach us — is approximately 93 billion light-years in diameter. Beyond that horizon, light has not yet reached us since the Big Bang. Whatever lies there is not, for us, observable. It may be infinite. It may contain other patches of space with different physical constants. We cannot know.

Dark matter shapes the large-scale structure of the cosmos, accounting for roughly 27 percent of its energy content. It clusters, it gravitates, and it deflects light — and yet it has never been directly detected. Dark energy, comprising another 68 percent, drives the accelerating expansion of space itself, pushing galaxies apart at an ever-increasing rate. Together these invisible components constitute 95 percent of everything, and we do not know what they are.

Why is there something rather than nothing? This question sits at the boundary where physics meets philosophy — and likely beyond the reach of either. A theory that explained the origin of the universe would itself require a foundation, and that foundation would require an explanation, and so on without end. At some point, every chain of explanations terminates in a brute fact: something that simply is, for no further reason.

The universe does not owe us an explanation. It does not owe us comfort, or symmetry, or narrative closure. What it has given us — through the strange and beautiful machinery of mathematics — is a partial description, accurate enough to build lasers and predict gravitational waves and navigate spacecraft across the solar system. That is, by any measure, extraordinary.

The strangeness of quantum mechanics, the warping of spacetime, the invisible dark cosmos — these are not failures of understanding. They are the actual texture of reality pressing back against the limits of a brain that never evolved to grasp them. The correct response is not retreat into comfortable ignorance, but precisely what science demands: look harder, measure more carefully, hold the strangeness without flinching.

· · ·
The universe has no obligation to make sense to you. That it does so, even partially, is the greatest gift it never intended to give.
